# Debate: Can We Detect Hallucinations from Model Responses Alone?

## Experimental Context

**Model:** `meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct`
**Benchmark:** Natural Questions (NQ), 1000 questions
**Protocol:** 5 responses per question — 1 greedy (T=0.0) + 4 stochastic (T=0.7, then T=1.0)

| Metric | Phase 1 (T=0.7) | Phase 2 (T=1.0) |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Mean disagreement | 5.2% | 6.4% |
| Exact agreement (all 5 match) | 81.7% | 77.8% |
| Hallucination rate (greedy) | 70.7% | 70.7% |
| Hallucination rate (stochastic) | 71–74% | 74% |

**Central observation:** The model's hallucination behavior is almost entirely determined by the prompt — whether a question elicits a hallucination or not is consistent across sampling runs, even at elevated temperatures.

---

## FOR: Response-Based Detection Is Sufficient

The experimental results presented here offer compelling evidence that hallucination detection is tractable from model outputs alone, without any recourse to internal activations or white-box access to the model. The key finding — that exact agreement across five independent samples reaches 81.7% at T=0.7 and 77.8% at T=1.0, with mean disagreement never exceeding 6.4% — reveals a structural property of the model's behavior: for the vast majority of questions, the model's response is effectively deterministic with respect to its factual content. This consistency is not incidental. It reflects the fact that the model's parametric knowledge, or its absence, is the primary determinant of whether a hallucination occurs. Sampling noise is a second-order effect. The implication is direct: if you can observe what the model outputs, you are already observing the primary signal.

This finding maps cleanly onto the theoretical framework underlying Self-Consistency (Wang et al., 2023), which demonstrated that majority voting across stochastic samples is a reliable proxy for correctness in reasoning tasks. The present data extends that logic to open-domain factual recall. When 81.7% of question-response sets yield exact agreement across all five samples, a simple majority vote is not just a heuristic — it is a near-lossless compression of the model's epistemic state. The disagreement cases (roughly 18–22% of questions) are precisely the interesting ones: these are the questions where the model is uncertain, sampling from a broader distribution over possible answers. But even here, the majority response across samples provides a principled estimate of the model's central tendency, and the variance across samples itself becomes an uncertainty signal. High variance implies the model lacks a confident factual representation; low variance implies it does. Both pieces of information are recoverable from text output alone.

The prompt-determines-outcome hypothesis implied by the data has a further important consequence for detection methodology. If hallucination is largely a function of the question, not of the sampling trajectory, then the problem of hallucination detection reduces substantially to question difficulty classification — a problem that is in principle solvable with text-level features. A model that reliably hallucinates on question Q and reliably answers correctly on question Q' is a model whose behavior is predictable from the semantic content of the prompt. This means that a trained text-level classifier, operating on either the question alone or on an ensemble of responses, could in principle learn to distinguish hallucination-prone questions from knowledge-secure ones. The 70–74% hallucination rate, held constant across temperature conditions, confirms that this is a stable, learnable signal rather than a noise-driven artifact.

The practical implications for deployment are significant. Activation-based detection methods require white-box access to the model, are typically architecture-specific, and impose substantial computational overhead — storing and probing intermediate representations at inference time is expensive, and the representations must be re-learned whenever the model changes. Output-based detection, by contrast, is model-agnostic, API-compatible, and can be implemented with nothing more than repeated sampling. Given that the present data shows disagreement rates below 7% even at elevated temperatures, the cost of this repeated sampling is modest: five samples is sufficient to obtain a highly reliable signal for the approximately 80% of questions where the model has a stable epistemic state. For the remaining 20%, the disagreement itself is informative and can be flagged for downstream handling — abstention, retrieval augmentation, or human review — without requiring any internal model access at all.

It is important to be precise about what this argument does and does not claim. Output-based detection of the kind described here is a detection signal, not a ground-truth oracle. The majority response could be a confidently held hallucination, and disagreement could arise from superficial paraphrase variation rather than genuine factual uncertainty. The 70–74% hallucination rate demonstrates that consistent outputs are not necessarily correct outputs. A complete hallucination detection system must therefore combine the consistency signal with a verification step — ideally against an external knowledge source. But this is a practical limitation shared by all detection paradigms, activation-based methods included: activations tell you that the model is uncertain, not that the majority answer is correct. The fundamental bottleneck is knowledge verification, not signal extraction. And for the purpose of signal extraction — identifying which questions are likely to produce hallucinations — the output-based consistency signal is demonstrably sufficient. The data here shows that the model's behavior is structured, stable, and legible from its outputs. That is all a detection signal needs to be.

## AGAINST: Response-Based Detection Is Fundamentally Insufficient

The experimental results presented here are striking at first glance: 81.7% exact agreement across five independent samples at T=0.7, rising to a clean statistical picture where the model's behavior is largely deterministic given the prompt. One is tempted to conclude that if a model reliably produces the same answer, that answer's correctness can be verified through self-consistency. This conclusion is precisely wrong, and understanding why requires distinguishing between *consistency* and *accuracy*, between a model that knows what it knows and one that is systematically, confidently mistaken.

The most fundamental problem with response-only detection is revealed by the hallucination rate itself: approximately 70–74% of responses are hallucinated, yet the model produces them with extraordinary consistency. This is not a model hedging at the boundary of its knowledge — it is a model that has learned to fabricate answers with the same fluency and confidence it brings to correct ones. Self-consistency methods, from the original chain-of-thought consistency literature to more recent semantic clustering approaches, rest on the assumption that correct knowledge is more stable than confabulated knowledge. This assumption fails catastrophically here. When a model hallucinates on 70% of questions and does so consistently across all five samples, majority voting does not surface uncertainty — it enshrines error. The method cannot distinguish between a model that has learned a fact and one that has learned a plausible-sounding confabulation. Both produce high agreement; only one is right.

The 18–22% of cases where responses *do* disagree are equally damning, but for a different reason. These are precisely the boundary cases — questions at the edge of the model's competence, where the prompt is genuinely ambiguous, where a real detector must make its hardest decisions. A response-based detector, having no signal on the majority of questions (where agreement is high but often wrong), is now also deprived of a coherent signal on the remainder. Disagreement across samples could mean the model genuinely doesn't know, or it could mean the question admits multiple valid phrasings, or the stochastic sampling is perturbing a correct answer unnecessarily. Without ground truth — which is exactly what we are trying to avoid requiring — these cases are unresolvable from text alone. Activation-based methods, by contrast, have access to the model's internal geometry during processing, and uncertainty is encoded in the structure of those representations regardless of whether the surface-level output varies.

The generalization problem is particularly acute given the NQ benchmark's structure. Natural Questions elicits short, factual, closed-domain answers: entity names, dates, numerical values. This is precisely the regime where response-based methods look most promising — the answer space is small, agreement is easy to define, and semantic similarity is trivially computable. Move to longer-form generation — medical summaries, historical analysis, scientific explanation — and every advantage evaporates. Hallucinations in complex outputs are not replacements of one entity with another; they are coherent paragraphs that subtly misattribute causation, confabulate citations, or blend facts from unrelated contexts in ways that are superficially indistinguishable from accurate summaries. Five samples of such text will agree on the wrong mechanism with the same linguistic confidence they show on the right one. The NQ results thus represent an upper bound on what response-based methods can achieve, and even there, the method fails on 70% of prompts.

Critically, the experimental findings confirm the user's hypothesis — hallucination is largely decided by the prompt — but this insight does not rescue response-only detection; it undermines it. If correctness is determined at the prompt level, then the signal we need is not in the distribution of outputs but in how the model *processes* the prompt internally. The activations at intermediate layers capture the model's representational commitment to an answer before decoding begins: which factual associations were retrieved, how strongly the model's internal states point toward a confident versus uncertain regime, whether the geometry of the residual stream resembles patterns seen during accurate recall or during confabulation. A response reveals only the endpoint of this computation. Asking whether a response is hallucinated from the response alone is like diagnosing a disease from the patient's self-report of symptoms while refusing to order a blood test. The patient may be consistently, fluently wrong about their own condition.

The deeper epistemological issue is calibration. A well-calibrated model that does not know an answer will express that uncertainty through hedging, qualification, or explicit acknowledgment of ignorance. Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct does not do this — it produces confident declarative answers whether it is correct or not, as the near-identical hallucination rates across all sampling conditions confirm. Activation-based hallucination detection works precisely because calibration failures that are invisible at the output level leave traces in the model's internal representations. Probing classifiers trained on intermediate-layer activations have repeatedly shown that models encode factual uncertainty in ways they do not express, that the geometry of the representation space distinguishes "retrieved fact" from "plausible generation" even when the surface text does not. Response-only methods are blind to this signal by construction. The case for activation analysis is not merely that it adds information — it is that it accesses the only level of representation where the causal antecedents of hallucination are actually present.

# Debate 2: Are Response Activations Sufficient, or Do We Need Prompt Activations?

**Question:** For hallucination detection via activation probing, do we need activations from prompt processing (A), response generation (B), or both (C)?

**Claim to evaluate:** Response activations (B) alone are sufficient — prompt activations add nothing.

---

## FOR: Response Activations Are Sufficient

The fundamental distinction between prompt processing and response generation in autoregressive language models is not merely architectural — it is causal. When a model processes an input question, it performs a read-only forward pass: the resulting key-value cache is a deterministic function of the prompt tokens and model weights, producing identical activations every time the same question is presented. The actual hallucination event, however, occurs downstream: it is instantiated in the generation phase, when the model commits to specific output tokens. A probing classifier trained to detect hallucination must therefore operate on representations that carry information about *which* answer the model produced, not merely which question it was asked. Response-phase activations are the only representations that satisfy this requirement.

This point becomes concrete when we consider the variance structure across samples. In the NQ multi-response experiment, five independent samples from the same prompt show 81.7% exact agreement on the generated answer, with a hallucination rate stable at 70–74% across runs. Crucially, prompt activations are *exactly identical* across all five samples — they contain zero within-question variance. A supervised probing classifier can only learn from dimensions that co-vary with the label. If prompt activations are fixed across samples of the same question, they contribute no discriminative signal for distinguishing hallucinated from correct responses within a question; any apparent signal would have to come from between-question variation, which conflates factual difficulty, topic, and question length with hallucination tendency. Response activations, by contrast, differ across samples even for the same question, and those differences are precisely correlated with whether the model produced a correct or hallucinated answer. This is the variance that matters.

A natural objection is that the residual stream during generation already *encodes* all prompt-derived information via the KV cache. This is not merely true — it is the key argument in favor of response-only probing. In a transformer with causal attention, each generated token attends to all prior context through the KV cache. The hidden state at every generated token position is therefore a function of the full prompt representation *plus* the accumulating generation context. Response activations are a strict informational superset of prompt activations: they contain everything the prompt activations contain, and additionally encode the generative commitment the model has made. Using both prompt and response activations would introduce redundancy — the prompt signal is already folded into the response residual stream — while adding unnecessary dimensionality and computational overhead.

The causal framing reinforces this conclusion. Hallucination is not a property of the question; it is a property of the generated response. A model that correctly answers "Who wrote Hamlet?" in four out of five samples is not hallucinating during prompt processing — it is hallucinating during a specific generation trajectory. The moment of hallucination is the moment the model assigns high probability to an incorrect token. The internal representations most proximate to this event are the hidden states at the generated token positions, particularly in the middle-to-late layers where factual recall and output commitment are thought to be localized. Prompt-phase activations are temporally and causally upstream of this event; probing them for hallucination is analogous to measuring a patient's resting heart rate to detect the moment of a cardiac arrhythmia — the measurement exists before the event and cannot reflect it.

The 81.7% cross-sample agreement further suggests that for most questions, the model's generative behavior is largely determined by stable internal biases — prior to any token being generated. This might seem to argue for the importance of prompt activations (since they precede generation), but the inference runs in the opposite direction. The stability of hallucination across samples means that the *response* activations will be similarly stable across samples of the same question: a model that consistently hallucinates will consistently produce response activations characteristic of hallucination, and a model that consistently retrieves correctly will produce response activations characteristic of accurate recall. This within-question stability makes response activations a clean, reliable signal, not a noisy one. The determinism works in our favor.

Practically, response-only logging reduces storage and compute requirements in proportion to the ratio of prompt tokens to response tokens — which for short-answer benchmarks like NQ can be substantial. More importantly, it simplifies the probing setup: there is a single, well-defined token sequence to log (the generated answer), a natural alignment between the logged activations and the binary label (hallucinated or not), and no need to design aggregation schemes over variable-length prompt prefixes. Prompt activations present a non-trivial pooling problem: which token positions to use, how to weight them, whether to use the last token or an average. Response activations, particularly at the final generated token or at a sequence-level aggregation of the answer span, have a clean semantic interpretation as the model's committed knowledge state. For a hallucination detector to be scientifically interpretable and practically deployable, response-phase activations are not merely sufficient — they are the appropriate unit of analysis.

## AGAINST: Prompt Activations Are Necessary

The high cross-sample agreement observed in the NQ experiment — 81.7% exact match across five independent samples, with hallucination rates stable between 70–74% — is not merely a curiosity. It is direct empirical evidence that the hallucination decision is made during prompt processing, not during generation. If response activations were the primary locus of the discriminative signal, one would expect hallucination behavior to vary with the stochastic choices made during autoregressive decoding: different token sequences, different attention patterns, different residual stream trajectories. Instead, the model's fate is largely sealed before it writes a single output token. The per-question consistency implies a per-question cause — and per-question activations are precisely what prompt processing produces. Response activations, by contrast, are one downstream realization of that decision, selected by sampling. A classifier built on response activations is therefore modeling an effect rather than its cause, which is a structurally weaker position for both interpretability and generalization.

A related architectural argument concerns what information is actually preserved in response-time activations. During autoregressive generation, the model accesses prompt representations only through the KV cache — a compressed, attention-pooled summary in which the full per-token, per-layer spatial structure of prompt processing has been collapsed. The KV cache entries for each attention head encode a weighted mixture of prompt token values, discarding the local representational geometry that would allow a probe to identify, for instance, whether the model's mid-layer representations of the question tokens successfully matched a stored factual trace in its parameters. Mechanistic interpretability work on factual recall — particularly Meng et al. (ROME, 2022) and Geva et al.'s analysis of the MLP layers as "knowledge neurons" — consistently locates the retrieval computation in the forward passes over subject tokens during prompt encoding. These computations produce the residual-stream state at the last prompt token, which functions as a summary vector carrying the retrieved (or failed-to-retrieve) factual content into the generation phase. Probing that vector directly is therefore a more proximal measurement of the retrieval outcome than probing any response token's activation, which reflects the result of retrieval only after it has been further transformed by the generation process.

Response activations introduce confounds that are orthogonal to the hallucination signal and that actively degrade classifier performance. Autoregressive generation produces activations that are a function not only of what the model "knows" but also of token position within the response, the particular lexical choices made at earlier time steps (which alter the attention context for later ones), and stochastic temperature effects. A classifier trained on response activations must therefore disentangle the hallucination signal from these generation-specific artifacts. This is a harder problem than necessary. Prompt activations, by contrast, are deterministic given the input: for a fixed question, the last-prompt-token residual stream is always identical, regardless of how many samples are drawn. The high cross-sample agreement in the NQ experiment means the oracle label is also essentially constant per question, so the ideal feature representation should itself be constant per question — a property only prompt activations possess. Using response activations from a single sample introduces noise relative to this ideal; aggregating across multiple samples partially recovers the signal but at multiplicative inference cost.

From a practical standpoint, response activations are unavailable until after the model has already generated an answer, making them useless for any application that requires pre-generation detection or intervention — for instance, retrieval-augmented generation systems that must decide whether to fetch external evidence before producing output, or decoding-time steering methods that modify the generation trajectory. Prompt activations are available after a single forward pass over the input, enabling zero-shot detection at the cost of one prefill. This is not merely an engineering convenience; it reflects a principled commitment to detecting the hallucination at its point of origin rather than auditing it post hoc. A detector that operates on prompt activations can, in principle, be coupled to intervention mechanisms (activation patching, constrained decoding, retrieval triggers) at the decision boundary — the moment before generation begins. A response-only detector arrives too late to be causally useful.

To be precise about what response activations do offer: they provide evidence about hallucination as expressed in the generated text, and certain generation-phase phenomena — such as the model's uncertainty as measured by entropy over the vocabulary at each step, or the coherence of the generated token sequence — do carry hallucination signal. Consistency-based methods (SelfCheckGPT, semantic entropy) exploit exactly this, and they are demonstrably useful. But these methods require multiple samples, they measure consistency of output rather than correctness of retrieval, and they remain downstream of the causal event. The NQ multi-response result shows that the per-question hallucination rate is stable across samples precisely because the underlying cause is stable — which is the argument for prompt activations, not against them. A detector that conditions on prompt activations captures the cause directly, in a single deterministic pass, without the noise, cost, or causal distance that response-only approaches incur. The sufficiency claim for response activations conflates correlation with causation and availability with optimality.

## Key Points of Contention

| Issue | FOR (response only) | AGAINST (need prompt) |
|-------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Where is hallucination "decided"? | At generation — the committed token is the event | During prompt processing — fate sealed before first output token |
| Variance argument | Response activations vary across samples; prompt activations don't | Prompt activations are constant per question — ideal since label is also constant per question |
| KV cache | Response activations are a superset — they contain all prompt info | KV cache is lossy; full per-token prompt geometry is discarded |
| Causal proximity | Response = the moment of hallucination | Prompt = the retrieval failure that causes it |
| Confounds | Prompt activations don't reflect which answer was given | Response activations entangle hallucination with token position, lexical choices, temperature |
| Timing / intervention | Detection after generation is still useful (auditing) | Prompt activations enable pre-generation intervention |
| Single-sample cost | One generation pass logs response activations | One prefill pass logs prompt activations — cheaper |
